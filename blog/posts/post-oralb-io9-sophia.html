What the iO 9 Actually Does — Features That Matter for Orthodontic Patients

Let me walk through the features I actually use and care about, because the spec sheet is long and not all of it is equally relevant if you're mid-treatment like I was.

The round oscillating-rotating brush head is genuinely better for getting around Invisalign attachment buttons than a sonic brush with a rectangular head. 7 Cleaning Modes — The Ones I Actually Use

The iO 9 comes with seven cleaning modes: Clean, Whitening, Gum Care, Intense Clean, Sensitive, Tongue Cleaning, and Orthodontic. I rotate between Orthodontic for my morning brush and Whitening for my evening session. Sensitive gets occasional use on days when my gums are irritated after a tray change. The other modes exist and I'm sure they're useful for someone, but I don't find myself reaching for Tongue Cleaning mode with any regularity.

  • Orthodontic mode: optimized pressure and motion for cleaning around brackets and attachments
  • Whitening mode: slightly more polishing action — noticeably effective over weeks of use
  • Sensitive mode: gentler oscillation for post-tray-change soreness
  • Gum Care: extended brushing time with focus on the gumline — I use this once a week

AI Coaching and the 16-Zone Mouth Map

The iO app connects via Bluetooth and divides your mouth into 16 zones, tracking where you've brushed and for how long. I was skeptical of this feature before I tried it — it sounded gimmicky. But the first time I used it, I discovered I was consistently under-brushing my upper left molars. I'd been doing that for years without knowing it. The app caught it in three days. That kind of real-time feedback is genuinely useful, especially when you're in orthodontic treatment and your dentist is watching your gum health closely.

The AI also monitors brushing pressure in real time, and the LED ring at the base of the brush head changes color — green for correct pressure, white for light, red for too hard. I press too hard when I'm distracted or rushing, and the red ring has saved my gums from unnecessary trauma more times than I can count.

Battery Life and Charging

The iO 9 charges via a magnetic charging stand and Oral-B claims up to two weeks of battery life per charge. In my experience brushing twice a day for two minutes, I get about 10 to 12 days before the battery indicator tells me to plug in. That's plenty. The travel case that comes with this model has a built-in USB charging port, which has been genuinely useful on work trips — one less adapter to pack.

What I Don't Love About the iO 9

I'd be doing you a disservice if I didn't talk about the downsides, because there are a few real ones.

First and most obviously: the price. $379.99 is the highest price point in the consumer electric toothbrush category. There is no getting around that. If you're not mid-orthodontic treatment with a specific clinical need for the features this brush offers, I would have a hard time telling you it's necessary. The Oral-B iO Series 5 or 6 would cover most people's needs at a significantly lower price.

Second: the replacement brush heads are expensive. Oral-B recommends replacing the head every three months, and the iO-compatible heads run about $10 to $15 each depending on which type you buy and whether you catch a sale. That's a real ongoing cost to factor in.

Third: the app can be annoying. It requires Bluetooth to be on, it occasionally loses connection mid-session, and it sends push notifications that I had to manually turn off. The coaching feature is genuinely valuable, but the app itself is not as polished as the hardware. That's a frustrating gap for a nearly $400 product.

Finally, the brush is heavier than I expected. It's not uncomfortable, but if you have smaller hands or any wrist sensitivity, it's worth noting.
